Homepage Configurator

Design Brief


One of the services ITS Warwick provides is to design page layouts for standard University websites. I was involved with the redesign and creation of an updated system that gave the end users more flexibility over their layout. The system also catered for my colleagues who would use the system to help walk customers through the design process, as well as to review customers' created designs.


Requirements


The application needed to work for two types of people, each with their own needs. For clients, the application acts as a form to request a new site layout. For content team members, it will be useful to use in front of clients to show design decisions in real time.


Clients

About

Clients have a huge range in technical capability. They will be guided towards a default design, but more experienced clients can make their own. They will also have to learn primarily through use.

Requirements
  • Able to infer meaning from visual feedback
  • Provide prompts and guidance to people who get stuck
  • Able to create any feasible design from given components

Content Team

About

Content team members are all technically competent. They are shown in person exactly how the application works. They will also be able to guide clients through the process.

Requirements
  • Able to load client's custom designs
  • Update the application later on to expand images
  • Separate documentation for content team actions

Research


In preparation, I looked online for similar systems to inspire me. I wanted to find tools that the clients are at least somewhat familiar with, to help them learn how to use my application faster.


LayoutIt!

There are many drag and drop website creators available, and I gathered that what makes these easy to use is abundant visual cues and limited functionality. The user is assumed to be someone that isn't so technically capable, so all of the decisions that require any development knowledge are removed. The word that comes to mind when you think of applications like this is 'easy', and that's what I wanted for my application.

Don't make them think Assume no technical knowledge Feedback for hovering items

Facebook

Facebook has a feature where you can select photos to create a video. Clicking a photo selects and deselects it. The limit is clearly shown and selected items are very obvious. The visual feedback is very clear; text is red if there is something important to say, and popups appear when you make invalid clicks. One of the keys to a good usable interface is feedback. Feedback gives the user an indication that the actions they take are affecting the system.

Do something on every action Add affordances Clearly show selections

Homepage Layouts

As part of my internship, I redesigned an even older version of the layouts picker. This version only countered for premades, but it also allowed the user to select a banner image. In order to keep consistency, I borrowed many ideas from my old designs.

Make consistent design choices Learn from mistakes Keep important features

Design


Initially, I started out with pencil wireframes. This allowed me to picture the layout more clearly. I then incrementally developed the configurator, amending design choices as I ruled certain options out. For example, originally, the bin was much smaller, but I found that it was harder to drag an item into it accurately. The decision was made to make the bin hitbox much larger to make removing items faster and a little easier.

Part way through development, I had a meeting with my co-workers who would be using this application in the future. They provided insight into what customers would find to be useful, and what else I could change to make their jobs easier.

Another big hurdle was making the site cross-browser compatible. Many of the clients are still using Internet Explorer, a browser that doesn't cope well with some of our standard methods. For example, the colour slider is only visible on compatible browsers to avoid confusion. A small tooltip appears at the top of the page if the user is on an incompatible browser.

Through design and implementation, the most important thing to do was to keep myself aware of how other people would see and use the application. I continually asked for feedback, and kept an open mind when making decisions.

Final Result


The final product fits its purpose and works well. Minimal effort is required on the user's part; as they select items, all of the form information is completed for them. It also provides the content team with a good tool to actively show customers in real time how their design choices will look.

I have created a product that is expandable and maintainable. Banner images and ready made designs are easy to add.

The final result. Components are dragged in from the left into the center area. The colour and number of columns can be changed.

Once a design has been created and saved, the screen is disabled to prevent retroactive changes

For choosing images, the following interface is shown. Selected images are indicated by a green banner.

The final form, completely untouched by a human. The user only needs to fill in their site name and location.

One piece of feedback is that clients get confused by mobile view. This feature allows clients to preview a potential mobile site.

The thumbnails for premade site designs are quite small. Taken from an older version of the form, a larger preview can be seen in a modal.
